10 UK Driving Laws That Just Changed in 2026

From AI cameras looking inside your cabin to mandatory speed limiters in new cars, expanding Clean Air Zones and the end of free EV road tax — here's what every UK driver needs to know right now.

1 AI Cameras Looking Inside Your Car

New AI cameras — mounted on vans and trailers — are being trialled across around a dozen UK police force areas including Devon and Cornwall, Sussex, Greater Manchester, Staffordshire and Durham. Unlike traditional speed cameras, these systems look inside your cabin to check whether you're wearing a seatbelt or holding a phone.

The AI flags potential offences, but every image is reviewed by a human officer before any penalty is issued. This is still a trial rather than a full national rollout — but it is expanding.

⚠️ Penalties where enforced: Using a handheld phone while driving — 6 penalty points and a fine of up to £1,000. Not wearing a seatbelt — up to £500. Pass your test in the last two years and 6 points means your licence is revoked automatically.
Law: Road Traffic Act 1988 — mobile phone and seatbelt offences. The AI camera system (Acusensus Heads Up) is the enforcement technology, not new legislation.
2 Pavement Parking Enforcement Powers

Scotland banned pavement parking outright in December 2023. England is now catching up — but not all at once. The English Devolution and Community Empowerment Act received Royal Assent in April 2026, giving local councils the legal power to enforce against pavement parking for the first time, taking over from the police.

This requires secondary legislation before individual councils can start issuing fines, so enforcement is rolling out council by council rather than switching on nationally overnight. Where it is active, fines are expected to be in the £60 to £130 range. Watch your local council's announcements — this will expand through 2026 and into 2027.

3 The 10% Speeding Tolerance Is Not Guaranteed

For years, UK drivers have relied on an informal "10% plus 2mph" grace period. The idea that doing 35 in a 30 zone wouldn't trigger a camera. That tolerance was never law — it has always been entirely at the discretion of individual police forces, and forces are increasingly tightening it.

With digital cameras now standard, there is effectively zero mechanical margin for error. A police force is legally entitled to issue a fine and points for any speed above the posted limit. Don't drive based on a margin that was never guaranteed.

Law: Road Traffic Regulation Act 1984 — sets speed limits. ACPO enforcement guidelines on tolerances are discretionary and non-binding.
4 Electric Vehicles Now Pay Road Tax

Free Vehicle Excise Duty for electric vehicles ended on 1 April 2025. From 2026/27, the standard VED rate is £200 per year for most EVs. New electric cars registered from April 2026 pay £10 in their first year, then £200 from year two onwards.

If your EV had a list price above £50,000 when new — a threshold raised specifically for electric cars from the previous £40,000 — you also pay the expensive car supplement: an additional £440 per year for five years from year two of registration. That's £640 per year total during that period.

Law: Vehicle Excise and Registration Act 1994 as amended. VED rates confirmed in Budget 2025, effective April 2025. ECS threshold raised to £50,000 for zero-emission cars from April 2026.
5 Acoustic Noise Cameras

Some UK councils and police forces are trialling acoustic camera technology that can detect illegally loud or modified exhausts. The system is triggered by decibel levels and links to ANPR cameras. This technology is still early-stage and not yet widespread, but if you have a modified exhaust it's worth being aware that enforcement is coming.

6 Smart Motorway Red X Automation

Red X lane closure signs on smart motorways are now enforced automatically. Gantry cameras can log your number plate instantly when you drive through a closed lane — no police presence required, no manual review needed before the penalty is issued.

⚠️ Penalty: £100 fine and 3 penalty points, sent automatically to your registered address. Always treat a Red X as a hard closure, even if the road ahead appears clear.
Law: Road Traffic Act 1988, Section 36 — failing to comply with a traffic sign. The Red X is a mandatory sign.
7 Roadside Eyesight Tests — Cassie's Law

Police have had the power to conduct roadside eyesight tests since 2013, but this power is being used more actively through 2026 as part of national road safety operations. If you're pulled over and cannot read a standard number plate from 20 metres away in good daylight, the officer can request your licence be revoked on the spot, with the DVLA notified electronically there and then.

⚠️ Penalties: Licence revocation, up to 3 penalty points, and a fine of up to £1,000. If you drive with glasses or contact lenses, you are legally required to have them with you and wear them every time you drive.

8 Mandatory Speed Limiters in New Cars (ISA)

Most new cars sold in the UK now come fitted with Intelligent Speed Assistance — ISA. Using GPS and traffic sign recognition, the system knows the speed limit on the road you're driving and will warn you, sound an alert, or in some models physically push back against the accelerator pedal if you exceed it.

You can override it by pressing firmly on the pedal, and you can switch it off. But by design, it resets itself back on every time you restart the engine. UK manufacturers have largely adopted this across their ranges in line with EU requirements, with a formal UK type-approval requirement expected from mid-2026.

9 Undeclared Medical Conditions

The DVLA takes undeclared medical conditions seriously. There are over 100 conditions you are legally required to declare — including epilepsy, certain heart conditions, sleep apnoea, insulin-treated diabetes, and several eye conditions. If you are involved in an accident and it emerges you had an undeclared condition, your insurance can be voided entirely.

⚠️ Penalty: Up to £1,000 fine and licence revocation. Your insurance is also at risk of being entirely void. If you're unsure whether your condition requires declaring, check gov.uk directly rather than guessing.
10 Expanding Clean Air Zones

ULEZ in London is widely known. The real trap in 2026 is driving into a different city without realising it has a Clean Air Zone. CAZ charges are now active in Birmingham, Bristol, Sheffield, Bradford and multiple other UK cities. There are no toll booths — just ANPR cameras reading your plate as you cross an invisible boundary.

Drive an older diesel or petrol car into one of these zones without checking, and you won't know about it until a penalty charge notice arrives through your letterbox a week or two later.

✅ What to do before driving to an unfamiliar UK city:

  • Check your number plate against the government's Clean Air Zone checker at gov.uk/clean-air-zones — it takes 30 seconds
  • If you're already facing a fine you think is unfair, check whether you have grounds to appeal before paying
Law: Environment Act 2021 — provides the framework for Clean Air Zones. Individual zones are established by local authorities under Transport Act powers.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can AI cameras catch you at a red light?

Yes. Holding a phone at a red light is still a driving offence — you don't have to be moving. The cameras don't distinguish between stationary and moving vehicles when detecting phone use.

Do Clean Air Zone charges apply to my car?

It depends on when your vehicle was registered and what fuel type it is. Most petrol cars registered after 2006 and diesel cars registered after 2015 meet the minimum emission standards. Check your specific vehicle at gov.uk/clean-air-zones before travelling.

Can I appeal a Clean Air Zone penalty charge notice?

Yes. Common grounds include incorrect vehicle data, administrative errors, or medical exemptions. You must usually appeal within 28 days of receiving the notice. Visit zoneappeal.co.uk to generate a formal appeal letter.

Is the 10% speeding tolerance still in place?

It was never law — it's always been discretionary guidance for police forces. Some forces still apply it. Others have reduced or removed it entirely. The safest approach is to treat the posted limit as the actual limit.

Do I have to declare a medical condition to the DVLA?

If your condition is on the DVLA's list of reportable conditions, yes — it's a legal requirement. You can check the full list at gov.uk/health-conditions-and-driving. Failure to declare can void your insurance and result in prosecution.
