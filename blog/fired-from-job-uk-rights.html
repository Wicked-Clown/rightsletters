Fired From Your Job? Here's What To Know Before You Sign Anything

If you've just been dismissed, made redundant, or handed a settlement agreement, the next 24 hours matter. Here's what your rights actually are in 2026 — and the mistakes that can cost you thousands.

Updated June 2026

Not legal advice. This page is general information. If your situation is serious or time-sensitive, contact ACAS or a qualified employment solicitor.

Don't Do These Things First

❌ Avoid these in the first 24 hours

  • Don't sign anything — settlement agreements, resignation letters, or disciplinary outcomes — without reading it properly
  • Don't hand back equipment or access immediately if you're disputing the dismissal
  • Don't send an angry email to your manager or HR
  • Don't post about it on social media
  • Don't delete work messages, emails, or leave WhatsApp groups

✅ Do this instead

  • Ask for everything in writing — including the reason for dismissal
  • Take time to read any documents fully before signing — pressure is not a legal deadline
  • Keep copies of emails, payslips, rotas, performance reviews and messages
  • Follow up any verbal conversation with a written email summary
  • Consider contacting ACAS before things escalate further

HR Is Not "On Your Side"

A lot of employees assume HR exists to protect staff. It doesn't — HR's legal duty is to the organisation, not to you individually. That doesn't mean HR staff are dishonest, but when HR says "let's keep this informal" or "we're just trying to help", remember who they're representing.

This is why getting things in writing matters so much. A verbal reassurance from HR carries far less weight than an email confirming the same thing.

Unfair Dismissal: What's Changing in 2026 and 2027

Your legal protection against unfair dismissal currently depends on how long you've worked for your employer — but this is changing.

Right now (2026): Most employees need two years' service before they can bring an ordinary unfair dismissal claim.
From 1 January 2027: The qualifying period drops to six months. If you started your job from July 2026 onwards, this protection applies to you once you reach six months' service.

Day-One Protections (No Qualifying Period At All)

Regardless of how long you've worked somewhere, some dismissals are automatically unfair from day one. These include dismissals related to:

  • Whistleblowing
  • Pregnancy or maternity leave
  • Asserting a statutory legal right
  • Trade union membership or activity

If any of these apply to your situation, the two-year (or six-month) rule does not apply — you're protected from your very first day of employment.

Settlement Agreements — Read Before You Sign

If you've been offered a settlement agreement, you're legally entitled to take independent advice before signing — and most settlement agreements only become legally binding once you've received that advice. Don't feel rushed into signing on the spot, even if HR implies the offer is time-limited.

Being Pushed Out vs Being Fired — Constructive Dismissal

Sometimes an employer doesn't dismiss someone outright — instead, hours get cut, duties change, criticism increases, and the pressure builds until the employee resigns. If you're being pushed toward resignation, don't resign emotionally. Document everything first — this could be relevant to a constructive dismissal claim.

ACAS Comes Before a Tribunal

Most employment disputes don't start in a courtroom. In most cases, the first step is ACAS early conciliation — which can often resolve a dispute before it ever reaches a tribunal. Employers often become noticeably more cooperative once they realise an employee understands their rights.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I be fired without warning in the UK?

In most cases your employer must follow a fair process — including warnings, an investigation, or a disciplinary procedure — depending on the reason for dismissal. Being dismissed with no process at all may be unfair, though your right to claim depends on your length of service and the reason given.

Do I have to sign a settlement agreement?

No. You're entitled to take independent legal advice before deciding, and you shouldn't feel pressured to sign immediately.

What should I do first if I think I've been unfairly dismissed?

Keep a written record of everything, gather any evidence (emails, messages, performance reviews), and consider contacting ACAS for early conciliation before deciding on next steps.

Does the two-year qualifying period still apply in 2026?

Yes — for most ordinary unfair dismissal claims the two-year qualifying period is still in place during 2026. It reduces to six months from January 2027.
